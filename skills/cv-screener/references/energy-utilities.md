## Energy-Specific Pass/Fail Criteria

### Hard Disqualifiers

1. **No sector experience for technical roles** — Energy is a technically complex sector. A candidate applying for a reservoir engineering, grid planning, or refinery operations role without any energy sector background is not credible.
2. **Upstream/Downstream mismatch** — These are distinct disciplines. An upstream production engineer applying for a refinery optimization role is a meaningful technical mismatch.
3. **No safety credentials for operational roles** — Offshore, operational, or field-based roles typically require specific HSE credentials (BOSIET, HUET, GWO for offshore; NEBOSH for operational safety). Their absence for roles requiring them is a disqualifier.
4. **No regulated market experience for regulated utilities** — Ofgem/FERC regulated network businesses require understanding of the regulatory framework, price control periods, and allowed returns. A commercial candidate with no regulated environment experience is a gap.
5. **No trading system experience for trading roles** — Energy traders and trading risk professionals must demonstrate experience with ETRM systems (Endur, Triple Point, or equivalent). Claiming trading experience with no system knowledge is suspicious.

### Strong Red Flags

- Renewables development CV with no project reaching FID or COD — a developer who has never taken a project through to financial close has an incomplete track record
- Energy transition language overloaded in a CV without any evidence of actual decarbonization work (projects delivered, carbon accounting, net-zero strategy implementation)
- Safety record not mentioned for operational/field roles — operational energy CVs without safety metrics or incident record context are thin
- Financial modeling claims for project finance roles without specifying model types (project finance, merchant, hybrid) or deal sizes
- Generic "oil and gas" background without specifying discipline (subsurface, surface, drilling, process, commercial) — this suggests limited depth
- No understanding of market structure for trading roles (balancing mechanism, spot vs. forward, cross-border flows)

### Quality Signals

**1. Asset Type and Scale**
What assets has the candidate worked on? GW-scale wind farm development vs. single-site 10MW solar is different. Major offshore platform operations vs. small onshore field. State the scale explicitly.

**2. Company Pedigree in Energy**
IOC experience (BP, Shell, ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Chevron) signals strong technical training. NOC experience (Saudi Aramco, Equinor, Petrobras, Adnoc) is sector-credible. Major utilities (National Grid, EDF, Enel, RWE, Orsted, Iberdrola) carry weight. Independent power producers and developers are credible depending on portfolio size.

**3. Proven Deal or Project Completion**
For project development and finance roles: has the candidate seen a project from origination to COD? For trading: specific markets traded (GB power, TTF gas, EEX spot, Henry Hub) and products traded?

**4. Technical Certifications**
Petroleum engineering (SPE membership), NEBOSH, Chartered Engineer status, IEng, CEnv, or equivalent sector-specific credentials add credibility to technical roles.

**5. Energy Transition Credibility**
The market is full of people claiming energy transition expertise as a buzzword. Assess whether the CV shows actual work: delivered renewable projects, implemented carbon reduction programs, authored net-zero strategies that were adopted. Claims without evidence are worthless.
